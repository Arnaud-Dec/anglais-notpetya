Presentation Plan: NotPetya – The Most Destructive Cyber-Weapon in History
I. Introduction
Presenters: Arnaud and Aiman.
The Hook (Question): “In your opinion, what is the most destructive cyberattack in history ?”
Opening Anecdote: * June 27, 2017: A silent computer in a dark office in Kiev wakes up.
A single, empty file named perfc appears and is moved to the Windows folder.
The next morning, the country wakes up to red screens, locked hospitals, dead ATMs, and paralyzed gas stations.
Subject Statement: We will analyze NotPetya, a 2017 cyberattack that started in Ukraine but threatened to become a global digital apocalypse.

II. Geopolitical Context: Ukraine as a Testing Ground
Hybrid Warfare: The attack occurred during the ongoing conflict between Russia and Ukraine that began in 2014.
Historical Precedents: Ukraine had already suffered two major attacks on its power grid in 2015 and 2016, leaving hundreds of thousands in the dark.
The Actor: Identification of Sandworm, an elite unit within the GRU (Russian Military Intelligence).
The Signature: Sandworm hackers were known for leaving references to Frank Herbert’s sci-fi novel Dune in their code.

III. The Weapon: Stolen Tools and Supply Chain Infiltration
The "Shadow Brokers" Leak: The most powerful tools used were not Russian-made.
The NSA Connection: The malware used EternalBlue and EternalRomance, high-end cyber-weapons developed by the U.S. National Security Agency (NSA) that were stolen and leaked online.
The Infiltration (Supply Chain Attack): * Hackers hid the virus inside a mandatory tax software update called M.E.Doc.
Since every company paying taxes in Ukraine must use it, it provided a "backdoor" into every major institution.
Propagation: The virus used Mimikatz—a tool created by French researcher Benjamin Delpy to show Windows vulnerabilities—to jump from one computer to another automatically.

IV. The "NotPetya" Mystery: Ransomware or Wiper?
The Naming Anecdote: * Initially, experts thought it was "Petya," a known type of ransomware.
However, researchers found the code was designed so that files could never be recovered, even if the $300 ransom was paid.
It was rebranded "NotPetya" because it was actually a Wiper—a weapon designed purely for destruction.
The "Vaccine" (Kill Switch): Researcher Amit Serper discovered that if a computer already had the read-only file perfc in its Windows folder, the virus would "think" it was already infected and stop.

V. Geopolitical Analysis: The "Why"
Strategic Destabilization: Launched the day before a Ukrainian national holiday to maximize symbolic impact.
The Cover-Up Theory: Many researchers believe the massive destruction was a "smoke screen" to erase digital footprints of earlier Russian espionage operations.
The Chernobyl Incident: The attack was so widespread it even disabled the radiation monitoring systems at the Chernobyl nuclear plant ruins.

VI. Results and Global Consequences
Uncontrolled Spillover: Because the internet has no borders, the virus infected any international company with anér office in Ukraine.
Economic Impact: * Maersk, the shipping giant, had 17 port terminals paralyzed, breaking global supply chains.
Total global damages are estimated at over $10 billion.
Lack of Accountability: Despite clear evidence pointing to the GRU, it took years for the international community to officially accuse Russia, and the impact of these accusations has been minimal.

VII. Conclusion
Summary: NotPetya demonstrated how a local political conflict can use stolen superpower technology to cause global economic chaos.
Answer to the Intro Question: No country is invulnerable; our interconnectivity is our greatest weakness.
Closing: There is no "rollback" to the innocence of the pre-2017 digital world. Thank you for your attention.

Key Vocabulary to Use:
Supply Chain Attack: Attacking a large group by infecting a trusted supplier (M.E.Doc).
Wiper: A malware designed to permanently delete data rather than hold it for ransom.
Attribution: The process of identifying the actor responsible for a cyberattack.
Non-kinetic warfare: Warfare that does not involve physical explosions or traditional weapons but causes physical damage.
Resilience: The ability of a system to recover from a major disruption.
